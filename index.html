Chloé Lavest

I am a PhD Candidate at Utrecht University. I am studying Perceptions of Discrimination of immigrants and children of immigrants in Europe - specifically in relation to their educational attainment (the Integration Paradox). I am supervised by Lucas Drouhot, Meta van der Linden, and Frank van Tubergen.

My research interests also include social stratification and social mobility, as well as intergroup relations, and perceived privilege from dominant groups in societies.

Ongoing Research

  • Beyond the Integration Paradox Highly educated minority individuals perceive more ethnic discrimination than their less educated counterparts – a now well-established finding referred to as the “integration paradox”. In this paper, we study whether this purported paradox captures general educational effects on the lived experiences of historically disadvantaged populations going beyond the scope of integration among immigrants. We do so by comparing educational effects on perceptions of ethnic discrimination among ethnic minorities with educational effects on perceptions of gender discrimination among women. We also study the discrimination experiences of the majority population, as well as intersectional dimensions in the experiences of minority women and those reporting multiple grounds for discrimination. Analyses based on representative data from two cross-sectional surveys in France - Trajectoires et Origines 1 and 2 – involving over 35,000 respondents, uncover a notable trend: highly educated individuals, including both ethnic minorities and women, are more likely to report discrimination, and that those who report discrimination on one ground are also more likely to report it on another ground. Our results suggest that the so-called integration paradox reflects more general educational effects on subjective experiences of exclusion that are not unique to immigrant-origin populations. | Slides
  • Growing up Disillusioned? Children of immigrants display high educational aspirations and expectations. This leads to the reduction of origin-related gaps in achievements, as children growing up in relatively more deprived families aim higher, aim more often for academic tracks, and stay in education longer than native students from similar socio-economic backgrounds. However, this can also lead to disappointment, when educational aspirations are not fulfilled. We study how unmet educational aspirations and expectations might fuel discrimination perceptions among children of immigrants in Spain. Following aspirations and achieved education across 8 years, from mandatory school to labor market entry, we identify when and for whom expectations turn sour. Using individual fixed-effects models, we show that disappointed children who prematurely leave the educational system display increased discrimination reports compared to their peers. | Slides
  • Who do They Think You Are? Estimates of intergenerational educational mobility are generally computed using a combination of self- and proxy-reports of one’s and one’s parents’ education. Such reports are easily collected, offering a cost-effective alternative to collecting multiple self-reports or register data. However, the bias that proxy-reports could introduce in the measurement of intergenerational educational mobility is rarely assessed. Our study fills this gap and assesses how reliable people are when they report their parents’ or their child’s educational attainment. We find that both parents and children tend to underestimate the educational distance between themselves and their family members, thus inflating estimates of educational reproduction. This trend is larger whenchildren act as proxy-reporters. Another limitation of using children’s proxy-reported information is the number of missing answers, which is lower when parents are asked to proxy-report their child’s education. In a simulation exercise, we establish that the bias introduced by proxy reports is not negligible, with self-reported intergenerational regression coefficients being 16% higher when a proxy-report is used. | Pre-print | Slides
